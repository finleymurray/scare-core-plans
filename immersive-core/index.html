The Haunting of Veil House

A Core Creative Group company. 100-guest immersive theatre inside a purpose-built Victorian mansion, housed within a 55m steel portal frame barn on 301 acres of Nottinghamshire farmland.

Vision & Concept

Not a show. A world you walk through.

Immersive Core exists to create theatrical experiences that blur the line between audience and performer, between watching and living. Our debut production, The Haunting of Veil House, places 100 guests inside a fully realised Victorian mansion where four unreliable characters tell contradictory stories about the same night. You choose who to trust. Then you discover none of them were telling the truth.

01

Narrative Architecture

The building is the story. Eight period-authentic Victorian rooms connected by corridors, staircases, and hidden passages. A Grand Ballroom for 100 guests. A Dining Room. A Séance Chamber. Every room is a scene. Every prop is a clue. The architecture controls pacing, reveals, and emotional beats without the audience ever feeling directed.

02

The Dual World

Behind every Victorian room sits a hidden observation laboratory, separated by one-way glass. Guests begin in the mansion. They end in the labs. The transition between these two worlds is the core reveal: the haunting you experienced was manufactured. The ghosts were actors. The supernatural effects were HVAC and projection. You were the subject of an experiment, not a guest at a haunting.

03

Year-Round Premium

Unlike scare attractions, immersive theatre isn't seasonal. Veil House runs 200+ shows per year, 4–5 nights per week, with premium ticket pricing (£55–75). Corporate and private events fill midweek and daytime slots. The experience is designed for repeat visits: with four characters offering four different perspectives, most guests see only 25% of the full story on their first visit.

The Experience

Four Characters. Four Lies.

100 guests arrive at Veil House for what they're told is a candlelit evening in a restored Victorian mansion. They're split into four groups of 25, each assigned a character who will guide them through the house. Every character tells a different version of the same night. Every version contradicts the others. The truth is worse than any of them admitted.

The Son

The heir who never inherited

Claims his father was murdered. Points blame at the Governess. His story is grief and rage, told through a study full of war memorabilia and a bedroom with uniforms he never wore in combat.

The Reveal

Behind the Glass

Every Victorian room in Veil House has a hidden observation laboratory directly behind it, separated by one-way glass. During the show's climactic third act, the mansion walls fall away and guests step through into the clinical world behind the performance. Suddenly they can see the room they just left from the other side. The ghost that appeared in the mirror was a projection rig. The cold spot was an HVAC vent. The rocking chair had a motor underneath it. Every supernatural moment was manufactured, documented, and observed.

The question shifts from "What happened in this house?" to "Why were we brought here to believe it?"

Victorian Side

Period-authentic rooms with gas-effect lighting, real antique furniture, practical props guests can touch and interact with, performer-led storytelling, and hidden theatrical effects (Pepper's Ghost, scent emitters, spatial audio, automated movement).

Laboratory Side

Clinical observation spaces with one-way glass, CCTV stills of the guests themselves, behavioural data charts, prop catalogues documenting every effect, and the exposed machinery behind every "supernatural" moment.

The Venue

Purpose-Built. Performance-Engineered.

Veil House is housed inside a 55m × 38m steel portal frame barn on the Cowsland Farm site. Two floors of themed spaces, connected labs, shared performance areas, and a dedicated external tech suite. The building is designed from the ground up as a theatrical instrument — every wall, corridor, and sightline serves the narrative.

The Barn Shell

Steel portal frame, 6-bay at 9.2m centres, 12m to eave, 14.5m to ridge. Composite cladding with full acoustic isolation. Mezzanine at +5.0m with 150mm composite deck rated to 4.0 kN/m² crowd loading. Internal environment fully controlled — the Victorian mansion exists as a building within a building.

Victorian Rooms (8)

Four character wings (A–D), each with a ground floor and first floor room. Son's Study, Governess's Parlour, Housekeeper's Kitchen, Medium's Chamber below. Son's Bedroom, Nursery, Master Bedroom, Library above. All period-dressed with practical props, scenic walls, and integrated theatrical effects.

Observation Labs (8)

One lab directly behind each Victorian room, separated by one-way glass. Clinical finishing: white walls, strip lighting, monitoring equipment. Each lab contains CCTV stills, behavioural data, and the exposed mechanics of the room's theatrical effects. Accessible from backstage corridors.

Shared Spaces

Grand Ballroom (22m × 12m, 100 guests) for the opening and finale. Dining Room (16m × 9m) for the dinner scene. Séance Chamber (16m × 9.5m, first floor) for the group convergence. Central corridors, twin staircases, and backstage routes connecting all areas.

Tech & Monitoring Suite

External building attached to the east face of the barn, approximately 100m². Houses the show control desk, CCTV monitoring (16+ feeds), audio mixing, DMX lighting control, HVAC management for cold-spot effects, and comms hub for performer earpieces. Direct access to the backstage corridor network via a covered link. Climate-controlled and acoustically isolated from the performance spaces.

Backstage Network

Dedicated corridors running behind all labs on both levels. Performer access to every room without crossing audience sightlines. Quick-change stations at key transition points. Two escape staircases. Fire-rated compartmentation throughout. The backstage network is the circulatory system that makes the cross-pollination narrative model work.
